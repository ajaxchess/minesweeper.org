How to Play Schulte Grid

Play Now Leaderboard Other Games

The Rules

  1. Choose a mode and a board size (3×3 to 10×10).
  2. A grid of N×N randomly arranged numbers will appear.
  3. Fix your gaze on the center of the board. The goal is to find each number using your peripheral vision — not by scanning your eyes around the grid.
  4. Tap 1 to start the timer, then tap 2, 3, 4… in order up to N².
  5. Tapping the wrong number does nothing — no penalty, just keep looking.
  6. When you tap the final number, the timer stops and you can submit your score.

The Six Modes

ModeWhat changes on a correct tap
NormalNothing — tile and number stay visible.
EasyCell background disappears; the number floats in space.
BlindThe number disappears; an empty tile remains.
Blind + EasyBoth tile and number vanish — empty space left.
Easy MixCell background disappears and remaining numbers shuffle positions.
MixRemaining numbers shuffle to new positions after each tap.

Mix and Easy Mix are the most challenging modes — the target numbers keep moving, forcing your peripheral processing system to constantly re-scan.

Board Sizes

Boards range from 3×3 (9 numbers) to 10×10 (100 numbers). Larger boards require more time and significantly more peripheral field. Start with 4×4 or 5×5 and work your way up.

Standard sizes used in training: 5×5 is the classic Schulte table and the most widely researched size. Athletes often train on 7×7 and 8×8 to push their visual field further.

Tips for Improvement

  • Don't move your eyes. This is the most common mistake. Looking directly at each number defeats the purpose — peripheral vision only develops when you train it under load.
  • Relax your focus. A soft, diffuse gaze rather than sharp focus lets you perceive a wider field. Athletes call this "soft eyes."
  • Practice daily. Research suggests 5–10 minutes per day over several weeks produces measurable improvements in reading speed and reaction time.
  • Increase board size gradually. Master 4×4 before moving to 5×5. A new board size feels dramatically harder at first but adapts quickly.
  • Try Mix mode. Even a few sessions on Mix will make Normal feel easy — the constant repositioning forces deeper peripheral adaptation.

Who Uses Schulte Grids?

  • Athletes — used in professional sports training to improve court vision, field awareness, and split-second decision making. Standard selection test for fighter pilots, racing drivers, and elite military roles.
  • Seniors — one of the most well-studied tools for cognitive maintenance. Regular practice supports attention, processing speed, and working memory as we age.
  • Readers and students — daily practice of 5–10 minutes can improve reading speed by 20–30% by widening the span of characters captured per fixation.
  • ADHD — the structured, immediately rewarding nature of the task helps build sustained attention and reduce distractibility.

History

The Schulte table was developed by the German psychiatrist Walter Schulte in the mid-20th century as a tool for measuring attentional and psychomotor speed. It became widely used in the Soviet sports science program during the 1970s and 1980s — coaches used it to screen and develop elite athletes. Today it is part of standard neuropsychological test batteries and sports performance programs worldwide.
